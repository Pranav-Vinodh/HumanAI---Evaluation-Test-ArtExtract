    \section{Task 1 -- Convolutional--recurrent classification
(WikiArt)}\label{task-1-convolutionalrecurrent-classification-wikiart}

\textbf{GSoC / HumanAI --- discussion notebook.} Here I implement
\textbf{multi-task} prediction of \textbf{style}, \textbf{artist}, and
\textbf{genre} on the ArtGAN \textbf{WikiArt Refined} setup: a
\textbf{convolutional trunk} (ResNet-18) on \textbf{horizontal image
strips}, aggregated by a \textbf{bidirectional GRU}, with \textbf{three
linear heads}.

I designed this to \textbf{train on resource-constrained hardware}
(consumer GPU or CPU-class laptop): a \textbf{small pretrained CNN},
\textbf{frozen backbone} by default, \textbf{subsampled data}
(\texttt{MAX\_SAMPLES}), and \textbf{short training}
(\texttt{EPOCHS\ =\ 5}). The sections below spell out \textbf{my
strategy}, \textbf{why I chose these models under that constraint},
\textbf{their strengths and weaknesses}, and \textbf{how I read the
evaluation metrics} after I run training.

\textbf{Data:} paths point to \texttt{DATA\_ROOT} / \texttt{CSV\_ROOT};
large assets are gitignored.

    \subsection{Approach and overall strategy (GSoC
discussion)}\label{approach-and-overall-strategy-gsoc-discussion}

\textbf{How I frame the problem:} Paintings are
\textbf{high-dimensional} and labels are \textbf{hierarchical /
correlated} (artist ↔ style ↔ genre). I use \textbf{one shared visual
representation} for \textbf{three classification tasks} so overhead
stays modest compared with training three separate models.

\textbf{My strategy:} 1. \textbf{Strip-based encoding:} I resize each
image to \texttt{224×224}, split it into \textbf{4 horizontal bands},
and run each band through the \textbf{same} ResNet-18 trunk. That gives
me a \textbf{short sequence} of feature vectors per painting. 2.
\textbf{Temporal pooling:} A \textbf{bidirectional GRU} reads that
sequence; I take the \textbf{last hidden state} to summarize vertical
structure (sky → ground, etc.) without a full 2D attention model. 3.
\textbf{Multi-task heads:} Three linear classifiers predict style,
artist, and genre from that summary.

\textbf{Why resource limits drove my choices:} Training a \textbf{large
ViT} or \textbf{unfrozen ResNet-50+} on full WikiArt would need
\textbf{more VRAM, longer epochs, and stronger regularization} than I
budgeted for. I \textbf{freeze ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-18} and only
train \textbf{GRU + heads}, which \textbf{shrinks optimizer state and
backward cost} a lot---appropriate for \textbf{my laptop /
\textasciitilde6--8 GB GPU} and \textbf{quick iteration}. Strip + GRU is
\textbf{lighter than a two-stage detector or transformer} while still
satisfying the \textbf{convolutional + recurrent} requirement.

    \subsection{Strip representation and recurrent module ---
trade-offs}\label{strip-representation-and-recurrent-module-trade-offs}

\textbf{Strengths I get from this design:} Four strips add a
\textbf{minimal notion of spatial layout} (top vs bottom of the canvas)
while \textbf{reusing one CNN} per strip---cheap compared with pyramids
or sliding windows. The GRU \textbf{combines} strip features with
\textbf{few parameters} at sequence length 4.

\textbf{Weaknesses I accept:} \textbf{Horizontal} slicing under-models
\textbf{left--right} structure, and resize loses \textbf{fine local
detail}. \textbf{Artist} identity often depends on \textbf{brushwork and
micro-texture} that 224px and strip pooling blur. \textbf{GRU vs LSTM}
barely matters at \texttt{T=4}; either is mainly a \textbf{small
trainable mixer} on frozen CNN features.

\textbf{What I would try with more resources:} Higher resolution,
\textbf{learned 2D pooling}, or \textbf{partial backbone unfreezing}
first---not necessarily a deeper RNN.

    \subsection{\texorpdfstring{Subsampling (\texttt{MAX\_SAMPLES}) --- why

metrics}{Subsampling (MAX\_SAMPLES) --- why it matters for metrics}}\label{subsampling-max_samples-why-it-matters-for-metrics}

\textbf{Why I cap samples:} Random \textbf{5k-row subsampling} keeps
\textbf{epoch time predictable} and stops \textbf{memory thrash} when
images load from disk inside the notebook. It is an explicit
\textbf{compute--variance trade-off} I document.

\textbf{Effect on evaluation:} Rare \textbf{artists} and \textbf{styles}
get \textbf{fewer training examples} after sampling, which usually
\textbf{hurts macro-averaged F1} and \textbf{artist accuracy} more than
genre. \textbf{Weighted} metrics (in \texttt{classification\_report})
stay closer to ``headline'' accuracy on \textbf{frequent} classes.

\textbf{What I state in my submission:} My scores are measured under
\textbf{these resource limits}; \textbf{raising \texttt{MAX\_SAMPLES}},
\textbf{epochs}, or \textbf{unfreezing the backbone} is the expected
path to higher numbers---not a separate algorithmic trick.

    \subsection{Model choice: ResNet-18 + GRU --- strengths and
weaknesses}\label{model-choice-resnet-18-gru-strengths-and-weaknesses}

\textbf{ResNet-18 (ImageNet, frozen):} - \textbf{Strengths:}
\textbf{Small} (\textasciitilde11M params in the full model; I only
train GRU+heads), \textbf{mature} pretrained features, \textbf{fast}
forward pass, \textbf{stable} optimization. - \textbf{Weaknesses:}
Features are \textbf{not art-specific}; a \textbf{frozen} trunk cannot
fix \textbf{domain shift} (paintings vs natural images).
\textbf{Capacity} may be too small for \textbf{hundreds of artist
identities}.

\textbf{Bidirectional GRU (hidden 256, 1 layer):} - \textbf{Strengths:}
\textbf{Low parameter count}; enough to \textbf{fuse} four strip
embeddings. - \textbf{Weaknesses:} \textbf{Limited expressivity} vs
cross-attention or learned per-strip weighting; \textbf{last-step}
readout may drop useful mid-sequence cues.

\textbf{Why I did not default to a Transformer:} At my \textbf{data and
compute budget}, ViT-style models raise \textbf{memory} and
\textbf{tuning surface}; I met the brief with \textbf{CNN + RNN} and
stay \textbf{honest about hardware limits}.

\textbf{next step} when GPU memory allows so textures can adapt to
\textbf{paintings}.

    \subsection{Training protocol and what to expect each
epoch}\label{training-protocol-and-what-to-expect-each-epoch}

Each epoch I run a \textbf{full training pass} then \textbf{validation}
with \textbf{\texttt{classification\_report}} per task. I use those
reports for \textbf{per-class precision/recall/F1}, not only accuracy.

\textbf{Under my resource limits:} \textbf{\texttt{EPOCHS\ =\ 5}} is
\textbf{short}; loss may still be \textbf{dropping} when I stop. In my
write-up I note that \textbf{longer training} or \textbf{early stopping
on val loss} would be standard if I had more time.

    \subsection{GSoC submission --- Conclusion (Task
1)}\label{gsoc-submission-conclusion-task-1}

\textbf{What I implemented:} Multi-task \textbf{CNN (ResNet-18 strips) +
bidirectional GRU + three heads} for style, artist, and genre.

\textbf{Hardware-driven choices:} \textbf{Frozen} small backbone,
\textbf{5k sample cap}, \textbf{5 epochs}, \textbf{modest batch size},
and \textbf{notebook-safe DataLoader} settings so the pipeline runs on
\textbf{typical student hardware} like mine.

\textbf{Strengths:} Clear \textbf{inductive bias} (spatial strips +
recurrence), \textbf{efficient training}, \textbf{interpretable}
per-task reports and confusion matrices, \textbf{outlier} inspection for
qualitative error analysis.

\textbf{Weaknesses:} \textbf{Generic} ImageNet features, \textbf{no}
art-specific pretraining, \textbf{limited} capacity for
\textbf{fine-grained artist ID}, \textbf{horizontal-biased} spatial
model.

\textbf{Metrics takeaway:} I lean on \textbf{macro-F1} to discuss
\textbf{rare classes}; I compare \textbf{three tasks} to show
\textbf{which label type} the representation supports best; I use
\textbf{confusion matrices and outliers} to argue \textbf{what I would
improve next} (data, unfreezing, architecture)---not only to report one
accuracy.
